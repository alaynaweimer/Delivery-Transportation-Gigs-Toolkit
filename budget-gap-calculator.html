üéØ Budget Gap Mode vs Max Earnings Mode

Budget Gap Mode is designed for drivers who:

  • Have a specific financial goal (pay off debt, cover rent shortfall, save for vacation)
  • Want to minimize vehicle wear and tear
  • Value their time and don't want to work every available hour
  • Prefer a sustainable, long-term approach

Example: Sarah needs an extra $800/month to cover her student loan payment. She works 10-12 hours per week during peak dinner rush on DoorDash. Once she hits her $800 goal, she stops. Her vehicle lasts longer, she avoids burnout, and she still has time for family.

Max Earnings Mode is designed for drivers who:

  • Want to earn as much as possible in a short timeframe
  • Are willing to accept higher vehicle costs for higher income
  • Have significant time available (30+ hours/week)
  • Understand and accept the tradeoffs (burnout risk, vehicle depreciation)

Example: Marcus needs to save $10,000 in 6 months for a down payment on a house. He goes all-in: 40 hours/week across DoorDash, UberEats, and Instacart. He tracks every expense, optimizes every shift, and accepts that his vehicle will need maintenance. After 6 months, he hits his goal and scales back to Budget Gap Mode.

üí° Understanding Your Budget Gap

Your budget gap is the difference between what you earn and what you need. Here's how to think about it:

Total Monthly Need = Monthly Expenses + Savings Goal

Budget Gap = Total Monthly Need - Current Income

If your budget gap is negative (you earn more than you need), congratulations! You're in a position of strength. You can use gig work to accelerate savings goals or pay off debt faster.

If your budget gap is positive (you need more than you earn), gig work can help‚Äîbut only if the math makes sense. That's where the rest of this toolkit comes in.

üöó The Vehicle Reality

Every mile you drive costs money‚Äîeven if you don't feel it immediately. The IRS standard mileage rate for 2024 is $0.67/mile. That includes:

  • Gas
  • Maintenance (oil changes, tire replacements, brake pads)
  • Repairs (inevitable as miles add up)
  • Depreciation (your vehicle loses value with every mile)
  • Insurance increases

Example: If you drive 500 miles/week, that's $335/week in vehicle costs ($1,340/month). If you're earning $2,000/month gross, your net after vehicle costs is only $660/month‚Äîbefore taxes.

This is why we separate Budget Gap Mode from Max Earnings Mode. In Budget Gap Mode, you minimize miles to preserve your vehicle. In Max Earnings Mode, you accept higher vehicle costs as a tradeoff for higher gross income.
